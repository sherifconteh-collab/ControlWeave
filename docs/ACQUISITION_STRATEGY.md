## Overview

Open-source status changes acquisition dynamics significantly. A buyer acquiring an MIT-licensed product gets limited competitive moat — anyone can fork the repo and compete directly. **AGPL + Commercial License creates defensible IP** that makes acquisition more valuable, not less.

The dual-license structure ensures:
- Any acquirer is buying the right to commercialize what the community cannot
- Competitors cannot simply fork the repo and resell the product
- Federal relationships and ISSM expertise embedded in the methodology are non-replicable assets

## Acquisition Structures

### 1. Asset Acquisition (Full Purchase)

**What it is:** Buyer acquires IP, codebase, brand, customer contracts, and roadmap. Full exit for founder.

**Mechanics:**
- IP assignment of codebase, trademarks, domain, and customer agreements
- Transition period (typically 6–12 months) for knowledge transfer
- Earnout provisions tied to ARR or product milestones common at this stage
- Existing customer contracts transfer to acquirer

**Who buys this way:**
- Strategic acquirers (GRC platform consolidators, cloud providers adding governance)
- Private equity roll-ups in the GRC / cybersecurity compliance space
- Defense primes seeking a commercial AI governance offering for federal bids

**Valuation range (2025–2026 market):**
- Early-stage (pre-$1M ARR): **4–8x ARR**
- Growth-stage ($1M–$5M ARR): **6–12x ARR**
- At scale ($5M+ ARR with federal contracts): **8–15x ARR**

**Key value drivers:**
- AGPL licensing moat (acquirer buys exclusivity, not just functionality)
- Federal pedigree (NIST RMF + DoD ISSM expertise embedded in the product)
- Framework breadth (NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act, CMMC, ISO 42001 — rare combination)
- Community traction (GitHub stars, contributors, organic pipeline signal)
- Gov Cloud path (FedRAMP-ready architecture is a premium in the federal market)

---

### 2. Acqui-hire

**What it is:** Buyer acquires the technology primarily to obtain the founder's expertise in federal GRC and AI governance. The founder joins the acquirer as a senior technical or governance leader.

**Mechanics:**
- Software IP transfers to acquirer
- Founder receives equity conversion + earnout tied to product milestones
- Title and role negotiated (VP of AI Governance, Head of GRC Product, CISO-level advisory)
- Retention period typically 2–4 years with vesting acceleration on change of control

**Who buys this way:**
- Large enterprise software companies building an AI governance practice
- Consulting firms (Big 4, management consultancies) acquiring GRC tooling + practitioner expertise
- Federal contractors building internal compliance platforms for CMMC/FedRAMP delivery

**Founder negotiation leverage:**
- Dual license means acquirer cannot simply fork the repo — they need the commercial license
- Federal relationships and ATO expertise are non-replicable — this is the moat beyond the code
- Acqui-hire terms should include: title (VP/CISO level), vesting acceleration on change of control, and product authority provisions

---

### 3. Strategic License / OEM (Retain Ownership)

**What it is:** A buyer embeds ControlWeave in their GRC suite, cloud platform, or managed service offering. Founder retains ownership of ControlWeave. This is not a sale — it is a partnership.

**Mechanics:**
- Exclusive or non-exclusive license for specific verticals, geographies, or use cases
- Revenue share model (typically 15–30% of revenue generated using ControlWeave)
- Or flat annual OEM fee ($150,000–$500,000/yr depending on volume and exclusivity)
- White-label rights available at premium (white-label removes ControlWeave branding)
- API-only embedding also available (lighter license, lower fee)

**Why this path is attractive:**
- Founder retains ownership and builds toward a larger exit
- Recurring revenue without dilution
- Exclusive license to a specific vertical (e.g., healthcare, defense) commands premium
- Non-exclusive provides multiple revenue streams simultaneously

**Who pursues this:**
- Cloud providers adding a governance module (AWS, Azure, GCP ecosystem partners)
- GRC platform consolidators who want a specific module (AI governance, RMF Lifecycle)
- Managed service providers (MSSPs) building a compliance-as-a-service offering

---

### 4. Private Equity / Growth Round (Retain Majority)

**What it is:** PE or strategic VC acquires a majority or minority stake to accelerate GTM. Founder retains equity and operational role.

**Mechanics:**
- Minority investment: PE/VC takes 20–40% stake for growth capital
- Majority investment: PE/VC takes 51–80% stake, founder retains equity and role
- Proceeds used for GTM acceleration (sales team, marketing, partnerships)
- Second close / full exit possible 3–5 years post-investment

**Typical terms at ControlWeave's stage:**
- Pre-money valuation: 6–10x ARR or 4–8x revenue run rate
- Board seat for investor
- Founder retains operating control through preferred stock provisions
- Anti-dilution protection

## Founder's Non-Negotiables (Any Structure)

Regardless of acquisition structure, the following terms are required:

1. **Product authority provisions** — Acquirer cannot immediately sunset the open-source community edition (minimum 3-year commitment post-close)
2. **AGPL community edition continuity** — The AGPL repository remains public and maintained
3. **Founder title** — VP-level or above, with product authority for the GRC platform for the earnout period
4. **Vesting acceleration** — Full acceleration of any unvested equity on change of control
5. **Customer continuity** — Existing customer contracts honored at current terms for their contract term
